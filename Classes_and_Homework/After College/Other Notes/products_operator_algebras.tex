\section{Tensor Products of $C^{\ast}$-Algebras}%
One of the main complications with $C^{\ast}$-algebras is that the algebraic tensor product $A\odot B$ may admit more than one $C^{\ast}$-norm. 
\begin{definition}
  A $C^{\ast}$-norm on $A\odot B$ is a norm $\norm{\cdot}_{\alpha}$ satisfying $\norm{xy}_{\alpha}\leq \norm{x}_{\alpha}\norm{y}_{\alpha}$ and $\norm{x^{\ast}x}_{\alpha} = \norm{x}_{\alpha}^2$ for all $x,y\in A\odot B$. The completion of $A\odot B$ with respect to this norm is denoted $A\otimes_{\alpha}B$.
\end{definition}
However, the existence of there being multiple norms is not necessarily universal.

For instance, since there is a $\ast$-isomorphism between $\M_n\left( \C \right)\odot A$ and $\M_n\left( A \right)$, we have that $\M_n\left( \C \right)\odot A$ admits a unique $C^{\ast}$-norm, which is the norm that it obtains from $\M_n\left( A \right)$.
\begin{definition}
  Let $A$ and $B$ are $C^{\ast}$-algebras. The \textit{maximal norm} on $A\odot B$ is given by
  \begin{align*}
    \norm{x}_{\max} &= \sup\set{\norm{\pi(x)} | \pi\colon A\odot B\rightarrow B\left( H \right)\text{ a cyclic $\ast$-representation}}
  \end{align*}
  for any $x\in A\odot B$. The completion of $A\odot B$ with respect to $\norm{\cdot}_{\max}$ is denoted $A \otimes_{\max}B$.
\end{definition}
This is akin to the universal $C^{\ast}$-norm on the group $C^{\ast}$-algebra $C^{\ast}\left( \Gamma \right)\subseteq B\left( \ell_2\left( \Gamma \right) \right)$.
\begin{definition}
  If $\pi\colon A\rightarrow B\left( H \right)$ and $\sigma\colon B\rightarrow B\left( K \right)$ are faithful representations, then the \textit{spatial} or \textit{minimal} $C^{\ast}$-norm on $A\odot B$ is given by
  \begin{align*}
    \norm{\sum_{i=1}^{n}a_i\otimes b_i}_{\min} &= \norm{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\pi\left( a_i \right)\otimes \sigma\left( b_i \right)}_{B\left( H\otimes K \right)}.
  \end{align*}
  The completion of $A\odot B$ with respect to $\norm{\cdot}_{\min}$ is denoted $A\otimes_{\min} B$.
\end{definition}
\begin{remark}
  If we consider von Neumann algebras $M\subseteq B(H)$ and $N\subseteq B(K)$, then there are numerous $C^{\ast}$-norms that $M\odot N$ can be endowed with, but their norm completions will not be von Neumann algebras. The von Neumann algebra tensor product is still well-defined, and denoted $M\bar\otimes N$, which is the von Neumann algebra generated by $M\otimes N\subseteq B\left( H\otimes K \right)$.
\end{remark}
There are a variety of technicalities we must consider before moving forward. First, we must verify that $\norm{\cdot}_{\max}$ is finite; for this, observe that if $\pi\colon A\odot B\rightarrow B(H)$ is any representation, then we may restrict to $\pi_A$ and $\pi_B$, such that
\begin{align*}
  \norm{\pi\left( a\otimes bq \right)} &\leq \norm{\pi_A(a)}\norm{\pi_B(b)}\\
                                       &\leq \norm{a}\norm{b},
\end{align*}
meaning $\norm{x}_{\max}< \infty$ for all $x\in A\odot B$.

The other major questions we must consider are the following.
\begin{enumerate}[(i)]
  \item Are $\norm{\cdot}_{\max}$ and $\norm{\cdot}_{\min}$ even norms?
  \item Is $\norm{\cdot}_{\min}$ independent of the choice of faithful representation?
  \item Is it possible to reduce the non-unital case to the unital case?
\end{enumerate}
We start by discussing the first problem. For this, consider the fact that $\norm{\cdot}_{\max}$ admits a universal property as described below, which follows from the fact that any $C^{\ast}$-algebra can be faithfully represented on some Hilbert space.
\begin{proposition}
  Let $\pi\colon A\odot B\rightarrow C$ be a $\ast$-homomorphism. Then, there exists a unique $\ast$-homomorphism $A\otimes_{\max}B\rightarrow C$ that extends $\pi$. In particular, a pair of $\ast$-homomorphisms $\pi_A\colon A\rightarrow C$ and $\pi_B\colon B\rightarrow C$ with commuting ranges induces a unique $\ast$-homomorphism on $A\otimes_{\max}B$.
\end{proposition}

If a group is acting on another group, there is a construction known as the (outer) semidirect product that enables this. We will construct a $C^{\ast}$-algebra emerging from an action $\alpha\colon \Gamma\rightarrow \aut(A)$, which will be dubbed $A\rtimes_{\alpha}\Gamma$.
